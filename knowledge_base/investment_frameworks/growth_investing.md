# Growth Investing: Strategies for Capturing Compounding Returns

## Overview

Growth investing focuses on identifying companies expected to grow their revenues, earnings, or free cash flows at a significantly above-average rate relative to the broader market. Unlike value investing, which emphasizes current undervaluation, growth investing is forward-looking — investors pay a premium today for the earnings power a business will generate tomorrow. The discipline requires rigorous assessment of addressable markets, competitive moats around growth vectors, and the sustainability of reinvestment economics.

Prominent growth investors include Philip Fisher (*Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits*), Peter Lynch (Fidelity Magellan Fund), and more recently, Cathie Wood (ARK Invest) and the teams at Tiger Global and Sequoia.

---

## The PEG Ratio: Normalizing Price for Growth

The **Price/Earnings-to-Growth (PEG) ratio** adjusts the traditional P/E ratio for expected earnings growth, providing a more apples-to-apples comparison between companies growing at different rates.

```
PEG Ratio = P/E Ratio / Earnings Growth Rate (%)
```

**Interpretation Guidelines:**
- **PEG < 1.0**: Potentially undervalued relative to its growth — a classic Peter Lynch signal
- **PEG = 1.0**: Fairly valued; the market is paying exactly one unit of P/E for each percent of growth
- **PEG > 2.0**: Expensive; the market is pricing in significant future growth or a premium quality business
- **PEG < 0**: Negative earnings — PEG becomes meaningless, requiring alternative metrics (EV/Revenue, EV/Gross Profit)

**Limitations:**
- Growth rate estimates are inherently uncertain; consensus analyst estimates often extrapolate recent trends
- PEG does not account for balance sheet quality, capital intensity, or margin structure
- High-quality businesses with durable moats may deserve a structural PEG premium (e.g., consistent 20%+ ROIC companies)

**Forward PEG** (using next 12-month EPS growth) is preferred over trailing PEG because growth investors are compensated for forward-looking bets.

## GARP: Growth at a Reasonable Price

**GARP (Growth at a Reasonable Price)** is a hybrid approach that blends growth and value disciplines. Coined by Peter Lynch, GARP seeks companies with:

1. **Above-average earnings growth**: Typically 15%–25% annual EPS growth
2. **Reasonable valuation**: PEG ratio below 1.5; not willing to pay any price for growth
3. **Strong fundamentals**: Positive free cash flow, manageable debt, high gross margins

**GARP Screening Criteria:**
- EPS growth rate: 15%–30% CAGR over 3–5 years
- PEG ratio: 0.5–1.5
- P/E ratio: Below 2x growth rate
- Revenue growth: Accelerating or at least stable
- Net margin expansion trend: Gross margins stable or improving

GARP avoids both the value trap (cheap for a reason) and the growth bubble (paying irrational premiums). It has been the most consistent approach across full market cycles, outperforming pure-value in bull markets while preserving capital better than pure-growth in downturns.

## Total Addressable Market (TAM) Assessment

For growth investors, understanding how big a company can get is as important as how fast it is currently growing. TAM represents the total revenue opportunity available if a company achieved 100% market share.

**TAM Calculation Approaches:**

| Method | Description | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Top-Down** | Start with macro market data, apply share assumptions | Established industries |
| **Bottom-Up** | Unit economics × addressable customer count | SaaS, marketplace businesses |
| **Value Theory** | Estimate value delivered to customers, price accordingly | Disruptive/new-category companies |

**TAM Analysis Framework:**
1. **Current TAM**: Existing market the company competes in today
2. **Serviceable TAM (SAM)**: Portion of TAM the company's product/geography can address
3. **Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)**: Realistic near-term market capture

**Red Flags in TAM Analysis:**
- Management inflates TAM by defining market too broadly (e.g., "We compete in the $10T global finance market")
- TAM that requires the company to expand into 5+ adjacent businesses simultaneously
- No clear path from current product to TAM capture (missing go-to-market strategy)

A company with a 1% share in a $10B TAM ($100M revenue) has much more headroom than a company with 40% share in a $500M TAM ($200M revenue) — even if the latter is larger today.

## Rule of 40 for SaaS Companies

The **Rule of 40** is the primary health metric for Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) businesses, balancing growth and profitability:

```
Rule of 40 Score = Revenue Growth Rate (%) + EBITDA/FCF Margin (%)
```

**Benchmarks:**
- **Score > 40%**: Healthy, investment-grade SaaS business
- **Score > 60%**: Exceptional; elite tier (Snowflake, Datadog at peak)
- **Score < 20%**: Concerning; question whether unit economics are sustainable

**Why the Rule of 40 Matters:**
- Early-stage SaaS companies sacrifice profitability for growth (high customer acquisition costs)
- The rule prevents overweighting either extreme: a company growing 80% with -60% margins is burning cash unsustainably; a company with 60% margins but 0% growth is stagnating
- As companies mature, the growth component compresses; the profitability component must expand to maintain a healthy score

**Complementary SaaS Metrics:**
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**: Revenue from existing customers after expansion/churn. NRR > 120% signals customers are buying more over time — a powerful organic growth engine.
- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) Payback**: Months to recoup CAC from gross profit. < 24 months is good; < 12 months is excellent.
- **Lifetime Value / CAC (LTV:CAC)**: > 3x is the industry benchmark for sustainable economics.

## Earnings Growth Acceleration

Growth investors prioritize not just the level of growth, but the *trend* in growth rate. **Earnings acceleration** — the rate of growth is itself increasing — is one of the most powerful signals in growth investing:

**Detecting Acceleration:**
- Sequential quarter-over-quarter revenue growth is expanding (e.g., 15% → 18% → 22% → 28% YoY)
- Gross margin expanding alongside revenue growth (operating leverage confirming)
- Management guidance conservatively set, leading to consistent "beat and raise" quarters

William O'Neil's **CAN SLIM** methodology explicitly requires earnings acceleration as a key criterion (the 'A' in CAN SLIM stands for Annual earnings growth > 25%; the 'C' stands for Current quarterly earnings acceleration).

**Earnings Surprise Effect**: Research shows stocks with positive earnings surprises outperform over the following 12 months (Post-Earnings Announcement Drift, or PEAD). Consistently surprising estimates to the upside indicates analyst models are systematically underestimating business momentum.

## Reinvestment Rate and Return on Incremental Capital

A growth company's intrinsic value depends critically on **how much it reinvests** and **what returns it earns on that reinvestment**.

```
Sustainable Growth Rate = Reinvestment Rate × Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)
```

**Reinvestment Rate** = (Capex − Depreciation + ΔWorking Capital) / NOPAT

**Implications:**
- A company earning 30% ROIC reinvesting 60% of earnings grows intrinsic value at 18% per year
- A company earning 8% ROIC reinvesting 100% of earnings barely covers its cost of capital
- The most valuable growth companies have **high ROIC and can deploy large amounts of capital** at those rates (Amazon Web Services, Visa's network)

**Reinvestment Quality Indicators:**
- Revenue per employee (rising = improving productivity from capex/R&D)
- R&D efficiency ratio: Revenue growth divided by R&D as % of revenue
- Capital intensity trend: Ideally falling over time as scale benefits kick in

## Growth Investing Valuation: EV/Revenue and Price-to-Sales

When companies are pre-profit or growing very rapidly, traditional P/E fails. Growth investors use:

**EV/NTM Revenue (Forward Revenue Multiple):**
- Enterprise Value / Next-Twelve-Month Revenue
- Software: Premium companies trade at 10–20x; median ~7x; value tier <4x
- Calibrate by growth rate: A company growing 40% deserves a higher multiple than one growing 10% with identical margins

**Rule of Thumb (Bessemer Venture Partners):**
```
Fair EV/Revenue ≈ NTM Growth Rate × Rule of 40 Score / 1600
```
(This is approximate; use industry comps to ground the analysis)

**EV/Gross Profit**: More appropriate than EV/Revenue when comparing companies with very different gross margin structures (hardware vs. software vs. marketplace).

## Risk Management in Growth Investing

- **Valuation risk**: High-multiple stocks are extremely sensitive to discount rate changes. A 1% rise in risk-free rates can compress a 30x revenue multiple to 20x.
- **Growth deceleration risk**: The market punishes decelerating growth harshly — a company missing growth by 5 percentage points may see a 30%–50% valuation reset.
- **Concentration risk**: Growth portfolios tend to correlate highly in risk-off environments. Diversify across sectors and growth stages.
- **Position sizing**: Use position sizes that reflect conviction AND your ability to endure drawdowns without forced selling.

---

## Summary

Growth investing rewards investors who correctly identify large, durable growth opportunities early and have the discipline to hold through inevitable volatility. The framework requires combining quantitative rigor (PEG, Rule of 40, TAM sizing, ROIC analysis) with qualitative judgment (management quality, competitive moat, product-market fit). The best growth investments are companies where the market consistently underestimates the size of the opportunity and the durability of the competitive position.

# The 2008 Global Financial Crisis: Origins, Collapse, and Recovery

## Overview

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC) was the most severe financial and economic disruption since the Great Depression. Its epicenter was the US housing market and the complex web of mortgage-backed securities that had spread credit risk — and hidden it — throughout the global financial system. The S&P 500 fell from its **all-time high of 1,576 (October 9, 2007) to a trough of 666 (March 6, 2009)**, a decline of **57.7%** over 17 months. Approximately **$11 trillion in household wealth was destroyed**. The crisis triggered a global recession, a credit freeze, mass unemployment, and a permanent reshaping of financial regulation.

---

## Origins: The Subprime Mortgage Machine

### The Housing Bubble (2001–2006)

Following the dot-com crash, the Federal Reserve slashed interest rates to 1% (2003–2004), making mortgages cheap and fueling a housing price surge. Several reinforcing factors created the bubble:

- **Loose lending standards**: Banks, knowing they could sell mortgages to Wall Street, stopped caring about borrower creditworthiness. "NINJA" loans (No Income, No Job, No Assets) became common.
- **Adjustable Rate Mortgages (ARMs)**: Borrowers took on loans with low teaser rates that would reset dramatically higher in 2–3 years — a time-bomb structure.
- **Home prices always go up (assumption)**: Risk models assumed national home prices could not fall simultaneously — an assumption that proved catastrophically wrong.

### The Securitization Chain

The crisis was amplified by a securitization machine that obscured risk:

1. **Mortgage Origination**: Banks and mortgage companies (Countrywide, Washington Mutual) originated subprime mortgages
2. **Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)**: Thousands of mortgages pooled and tranched; senior tranches rated AAA by Moody's and S&P despite containing subprime exposure
3. **Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs)**: BBB-rated MBS tranches re-packaged into new CDOs, with the senior tranche again receiving AAA ratings — "laundering" risk through complexity
4. **CDO²**: CDOs of CDOs — another layer of obfuscation. Risk was so dispersed and opaque that no single institution fully understood what they held.
5. **Credit Default Swaps (CDS)**: Insurance contracts on MBS and CDO default. AIG sold hundreds of billions in CDS protection without adequate capital — a fatal miscalculation.

### Rating Agency Failure

Moody's, S&P, and Fitch rated mortgage-backed securities using models that assumed only regional (never national) housing price declines. The rating agencies were also paid by the issuers they rated — a profound conflict of interest. When housing prices fell nationally in 2006–2007, these AAA-rated instruments became toxic.

## The Collapse: 2007–2008

### Early Warning Signs (2007)

- **February 2007**: HSBC took $10.5B in subprime-related charges — the first major sign of systemic stress
- **June 2007**: Two Bear Stearns hedge funds (leveraged subprime exposure) collapsed
- **August 2007**: BNP Paribas froze three funds, citing inability to value MBS positions — triggered the initial interbank credit freeze
- **September 2007**: UK's Northern Rock experienced the first bank run in Britain in 150 years

### The Lehman Brothers Failure (September 15, 2008)

Lehman Brothers, the fourth-largest US investment bank (with $639 billion in assets and $619 billion in debt), filed for bankruptcy on September 15, 2008 — the largest bankruptcy in US history. The government's decision NOT to bail out Lehman (contrasting with Bear Stearns' government-facilitated rescue in March 2008) triggered:

- **Money market fund "breaking the buck"**: Reserve Primary Fund fell below $1 NAV due to Lehman paper exposure — triggered mass redemption panic across money market funds
- **Commercial paper market freeze**: Companies could not roll short-term financing; credit markets seized globally
- **TED Spread** (measure of interbank credit stress) spiked to 4.65% — the highest since 1987, signaling extreme interbank distrust

### Market Impact

- **S&P 500**: 1,576 (Oct 2007) → 666 (March 2009): **-57.7%**
- **Financial sector**: Down 80%+ from peak
- **Bank stocks**: Citigroup fell from $55 to $1; Bank of America from $54 to $3
- **Credit markets**: Investment-grade corporate bonds yielded 7%+ above Treasuries (normal spread: ~1–2%)

## The Government and Fed Response

### TARP (Troubled Asset Relief Program)

Passed October 3, 2008 after initial Congressional rejection caused a 777-point Dow drop. **$700 billion authorized**; $431 billion actually used:

- **Capital Purchase Program**: $250 billion injected directly into banks (equity stakes in Citi, Bank of America, JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, etc.)
- **Auto Industry Bailout**: GM and Chrysler received $80B+
- **AIG Bailout**: $182 billion in total government support to prevent collapse of the world's largest insurer; AIG's CDS book was a systemic threat
- **TARP ultimately generated a profit for taxpayers** of approximately $15 billion — the recapitalization worked

### Federal Reserve Actions

- **Emergency rate cuts**: Fed funds rate cut from 5.25% to 0%–0.25% (December 2008)
- **Quantitative Easing (QE1)**: $1.75 trillion in asset purchases (MBS and Treasuries) to unclog credit markets
- **Term Asset-Backed Securities Loan Facility (TALF)**: Supported lending for consumer and small business loans
- **Backstop of money market funds**: Guaranteed $3.8 trillion in money market fund assets to prevent a systemic run

## Transmission to the Real Economy

The financial crisis transmitted to Main Street through several channels:
- **Credit tightening**: Bank lending standards tightened dramatically; small businesses and consumers lost access to credit
- **Wealth effect**: Household net worth fell $13 trillion; spending contracted
- **Housing collapse**: US home prices fell 33% nationally (Case-Shiller) — 50%+ in epicenters like Las Vegas and Miami
- **Unemployment**: US unemployment rose from 4.7% (November 2007) to 10.0% (October 2009) — 8.7 million jobs lost

## Value Investors' Contrarian Moves

Warren Buffett famously wrote an op-ed in the New York Times in October 2008: **"Buy American. I Am."** His notable actions:

- **Goldman Sachs investment (September 2008)**: $5 billion preferred stock at 10% dividend + warrants to buy $5B in common at $115 — ultimately profited ~$3.7 billion
- **General Electric investment (October 2008)**: $3 billion in GE preferred at 10% dividend
- **Wells Fargo accumulation**: Buffett aggressively added to Berkshire's Wells Fargo position in 2008–2009 at depressed prices

Other contrarians:
- **John Paulson** bet against subprime via CDS before the crisis — made ~$15 billion in 2007 ("The Greatest Trade Ever")
- **Michael Burry (Scion Capital)** similarly profited from betting against mortgage securities (depicted in *The Big Short*)

## Regulatory Reform: Dodd-Frank (2010)

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act was the most sweeping financial reform since the 1930s:

- **Volcker Rule**: Prohibited banks from proprietary trading and limited hedge fund/PE investments
- **FSOC (Financial Stability Oversight Council)**: New body to identify systemic risks
- **OFR (Office of Financial Research)**: Data collection and research on systemic risk
- **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)**: New agency protecting consumers from predatory financial products
- **Derivatives Reform**: OTC derivatives moved to central clearing and exchange trading
- **SIFI Designation**: Systemically Important Financial Institutions subject to enhanced supervision and capital requirements
- **Living Wills**: Large banks required to maintain resolution plans

## Long-Term Consequences and Lessons

1. **Complexity hides risk**: CDO² and similar instruments were so complex that even sophisticated investors couldn't assess their true risk. Complexity should trigger higher, not lower, risk premiums.
2. **Leverage amplifies everything**: Lehman was leveraged 30:1 — a 3.3% decline in asset values wiped out equity. Leverage is a crisis accelerant.
3. **Correlation spikes in crises**: Diversified portfolios across different credit products offered no protection when all credit assets fell simultaneously.
4. **Systemic risk requires systemic thinking**: Individual institutions deemed safe collectively created system-level fragility. Macroprudential supervision is essential.
5. **Counter-cyclical investing works**: Buffett's willingness to buy when fear was highest generated exceptional returns. Fear-based selling at troughs locks in losses.

---

## Summary

The 2008 GFC exposed the dangers of unchecked financial innovation, regulatory arbitrage, leverage, and opacity. The securitization chain that spread risk also spread it in ways that made it unmanageable. The crisis response — unprecedented in scale and creativity — ultimately stabilized the system, but the recovery was long and uneven. For investors, the crisis reinforced that maintaining liquidity, avoiding excessive leverage, understanding counterparty risk, and having the psychological fortitude to act contrarily during panics are essential competencies for long-run investment success.

# The 2022 Bear Market: Inflation, Rate Hikes, and Valuation Reset

## Overview

The 2022 bear market was characterized by a unique combination of the highest inflation in 40 years, the most aggressive Federal Reserve tightening cycle in decades, and a dramatic derating of growth and technology stocks that had benefited from the ultra-low rate environment of 2020–2021. The S&P 500 fell **-19.4%** for the year (technically narrowly missing the -20% bear market threshold for the full year, though it breached it intraday). The **NASDAQ Composite fell -33.1%** — its worst year since 2008. The **Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index fell -13.0%** — the worst year for bonds in the modern era. For the first time since 1969, both stocks AND bonds fell more than 10% in the same calendar year, devastating the traditional 60/40 portfolio.

## The Inflation Surge: 40-Year High

### CPI Trajectory

| Month | CPI (YoY) |
|-------|-----------|
| January 2021 | 1.4% |
| June 2021 | 5.4% |
| December 2021 | 7.0% |
| **June 2022** | **9.1%** (peak — highest since November 1981) |
| December 2022 | 6.5% |

### Causes of the 2021–2022 Inflation Surge

1. **Demand shock**: $5+ trillion in fiscal stimulus (CARES Act, American Rescue Plan) combined with pent-up demand post-lockdowns created a demand surge
2. **Supply disruption**: COVID-era supply chain disruptions (chip shortages, port backlogs, container shortages) reduced supply simultaneously
3. **Labor market tightness**: "Great Resignation" reduced labor supply; wages accelerated (+5%+ YoY by 2022), feeding service inflation
4. **Energy price spike**: Russia's invasion of Ukraine (February 24, 2022) drove oil from $75 to $130/barrel (March 2022); natural gas prices surged in Europe
5. **"Transitory" misjudgment**: Fed Chair Powell's characterization of inflation as "transitory" in 2021 led to policy being too accommodative for too long, allowing inflation to become embedded

### Core vs. Headline Inflation

- **Headline CPI** (includes food and energy): Peak 9.1% (June 2022)
- **Core CPI** (excludes food/energy): Peaked at 6.6% (September 2022) — more persistent and harder to contain
- **PCE (Fed's Preferred Measure)**: Peak 7.0% (June 2022); core PCE peak 5.4% (February 2023)

### Fed Balance Sheet Reduction (Quantitative Tightening)

Beginning June 2022, the Fed allowed $95 billion per month in maturing bonds to roll off its balance sheet, reducing it from $9 trillion toward $7 trillion — a reversal of years of QE-driven expansion.

## Market Impact: Growth Stock Valuation Collapse

### The Mathematical Relationship: Rates and DCF Valuation

Rising interest rates directly compress the present value of future cash flows:

```
Present Value = Future Cash Flow / (1 + Discount Rate)^n
```

For long-duration growth stocks (where most cash flows are many years away):
- A 3% rise in discount rate destroys a much larger proportion of value in a company with earnings 10 years away vs. a company with near-term earnings
- **Illustrative example**: $1 in earnings 10 years away, discounted at 3% = $0.74; at 6% = $0.56; at 9% = $0.42. A 6-point rate rise destroys 43% of the present value.

This explains why high-multiple growth stocks suffered far more than low-multiple value stocks in 2022.

## Cryptocurrency Collapse: Terra/Luna and FTX

2022 witnessed a simultaneous collapse in crypto markets, compounding losses for retail investors who had embraced digital assets during 2020–2021:

### Terra/Luna (May 2022)
- **TerraUSD (UST)**: Algorithmic stablecoin supposedly pegged to $1.00
- **Luna**: Sister token that maintained UST's peg through a mint/burn mechanism
- **Collapse**: UST depegged; the mint/burn mechanism triggered a hyperinflationary spiral in Luna; both collapsed to near zero in 72 hours
- **Losses**: $45 billion in market cap destroyed; triggered broader crypto contagion (Celsius, Three Arrows Capital, Voyager Digital all failed)

### FTX (November 2022)
- **FTX** was the world's third-largest cryptocurrency exchange, founded by Sam Bankman-Fried (SBF)
- **CoinDesk revelation** (November 2, 2022): FTX's balance sheet was dominated by illiquid FTT token; Alameda Research (SBF's trading firm) held $5.8B in FTT
- **Binance withdrawal and collapse**: Binance announced it would sell its FTT holdings; FTX faced a bank run; $6B in withdrawals in 72 hours could not be met
- **FTX filed for bankruptcy** November 11, 2022; $8–$10 billion in customer funds had been misappropriated
- **Total crypto market cap**: Fell from $3 trillion (November 2021) to $800 billion by end of 2022 (-73%)
- **Bitcoin**: Fell from $69,000 (November 2021) to $16,000 (November 2022) (-77%)

## Energy Stocks: The Outlier in 2022

Energy was the standout sector performer, driven by:
- **Russia/Ukraine war**: Sanctioned Russian oil and gas exports, reducing global supply
- **Post-COVID demand recovery**: Global oil demand returning to pre-pandemic levels
- **Years of underinvestment**: Low oil prices (2015–2021) had reduced exploration capex; supply couldn't rapidly expand
- **ESG constraint on capital**: Institutional ESG mandates had reduced financing available for fossil fuel expansion

**Key beneficiaries**:
- Exxon Mobil (XOM): +87%
- Chevron (CVX): +53%
- ConocoPhillips (COP): +67%
- Pioneer Natural Resources (PXD): +59%

Energy valuations remained low relative to earnings (P/E of 8–12x) even after large gains — a genuine value opportunity that the market was late to recognize due to ESG-driven divestment.

## How to Select Stocks in a High-Rate Environment

The 2022 experience validated several stock-picking principles for rising rate environments:

### Value Over Growth
- Low P/E and low duration stocks outperform when rates rise
- Earnings yield (1/P/E) becomes directly competitive with bond yields
- At 5% Treasury yields, a stock must offer a comparable earnings yield (P/E < 20x) to be competitive

### Characteristics of Outperformers in High-Rate Environment

| Characteristic | Rationale |
|----------------|-----------|
| **Positive free cash flow** | Not dependent on capital markets for survival |
| **Low debt / floating rate exposure** | High leverage becomes deadly when rates rise |
| **Near-term earnings** | Less sensitive to discount rate changes |
| **Pricing power** | Can pass inflation through; protects margins |
| **Energy / commodity exposure** | Inflation hedge; structural supply constraints |
| **Financials (banks)** | Net interest margins expand with rates |

### Value Stock Rotation in 2022

The Fama-French Value factor (HML) had its best year since 2000 in 2022. The Russell 1000 Value index (-7.4%) dramatically outperformed the Russell 1000 Growth index (-29.4%) — a 22 percentage point spread.

Banks, insurance companies, and industrial companies all benefited as the economy remained stronger than feared while rate-sensitive growth stocks collapsed.

## The 60/40 Portfolio's Worst Year in Decades

The traditional 60% stocks / 40% bonds portfolio fell approximately **-16% to -17%** in 2022 — the worst year since 1937. The diversification benefit of bonds failed because:
- Bonds fell due to inflation and rate hikes (bond prices fall when yields rise)
- Stocks fell simultaneously due to the same rate hikes
- The negative stock-bond correlation that had held for 20+ years (since 2000) broke down as inflation returned

**Implication**: A simple 60/40 portfolio is only a true diversifier in a low-inflation, declining-rate environment. When inflation is the primary risk, both stocks and bonds suffer.

**Alternatives that worked in 2022**:
- Commodities / energy stocks
- Trend-following (CTA) strategies: Average +20% in 2022
- Short-duration bonds (6-month T-bills)
- Long/short equity (value long, growth short)
- I-Bonds (US government inflation-linked, capped at $10K/person)

## Key Lessons from the 2022 Bear Market

1. **Interest rate risk is real**: Duration matters. Long-duration assets (growth stocks, long bonds) are highly sensitive to rate changes.
2. **Inflation is a systemic risk**: A regime shift in inflation breaks the stock-bond negative correlation — the foundation of traditional diversification.
3. **Valuation extremes revert**: ARK Innovation fell 75% from its peak. Companies with no near-term earnings path cannot be valued solely on TAM narratives.
4. **Energy/commodity under-ownership was an opportunity**: ESG-driven divestment created value opportunities in sectors the market systematically underweighted.
5. **TINA is conditional**: "There Is No Alternative" (to stocks) only holds when rates are near zero. At 5% risk-free rates, stocks must justify their valuation with real earnings yield.
6. **Cash is a strategic asset**: In a rising-rate environment, holding short-term Treasuries (risk-free 5%+ yield) outperformed most asset classes — the highest cash return in 15 years.

---

## Summary

The 2022 bear market was a regime change — from the deflationary, low-rate, liquidity-abundant era of 2010–2021 to a higher-inflation, higher-rate, tighter-liquidity environment. This shift required a fundamental reassessment of portfolio construction, valuation frameworks, and sector exposures. The math of discounted cash flow made growth stocks vulnerable; the math of supply and demand made energy stocks valuable. For long-term investors, 2022 reinforced that no single investment style (growth, value, bonds) wins in all environments, and that true portfolio resilience requires diversification across economic regimes, not just across correlated risk assets.
